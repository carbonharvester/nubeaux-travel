East African Coast Guide: Lamu to Zanzibar

January 10, 2026 16 min read

The Swahili Coast stretches over 3,000 kilometers from Somalia to Mozambique, but its heart beats between Lamu and Zanzibar. This is where African, Arab, and Indian influences have merged over centuries, creating a unique coastal culture of carved wooden doors, dhow sails at sunset, and the scent of spices drifting through narrow alleyways.

For travelers seeking something beyond typical beach destinations, East Africa's Indian Ocean coast offers living history, uncrowded shores, and experiences that connect you to one of the world's great trading cultures.

Understanding the Swahili Coast

The Swahili civilization emerged from maritime trade that connected East Africa with the Arabian Peninsula, Persia, India, and beyond. For over a thousand years, monsoon winds carried merchants, ideas, and goods across the Indian Ocean. The result is a distinctive culture—neither purely African nor Arab—expressed through language, architecture, cuisine, and customs that feel uniquely of this place.

Today, UNESCO-protected Stone Towns, crumbling ruins of ancient ports, and communities that still build dhows by hand preserve this heritage alongside modern beach resorts.

Kenya's Coastal Destinations

Lamu Archipelago

Time moves differently in Lamu. The UNESCO World Heritage-listed Stone Town has no motorized vehicles—donkeys and boats remain the primary transport. Narrow streets reveal centuries-old architecture, while Shela village offers pristine beaches and boutique hotels. The Maulidi festival each year celebrates the Prophet Muhammad's birthday with traditional poetry, dance, and dhow races.

Lamu rewards slow travel. Spend mornings exploring the labyrinthine old town, afternoons on rooftop terraces watching dhows cross the channel, and evenings dining on fresh seafood prepared with Swahili spices.

UNESCO Heritage Car-Free Beach + Culture 3-5 Days Ideal

Kilifi

Kilifi represents Kenya's emerging coastal destination. Set along a tidal creek where the Kilifi Bridge spans turquoise waters, this relaxed town attracts creatives, yoga practitioners, and travelers seeking alternatives to conventional beach resorts. The surrounding area includes ancient Swahili ruins at Mnarani and pristine marine areas at Watamu.

Stay at boutique properties like Distant Relatives (backpacker-meets-design) or exclusive escapes like Kilifi Bay Beach Resort. The scene is young, international, and genuinely laid-back.

Emerging Scene Wellness Focus Marine Parks 3-4 Days Ideal

Diani Beach

Kenya's most developed beach destination stretches for 10 kilometers south of Mombasa. While more resort-oriented than Lamu or Kilifi, Diani offers excellent value, reliable infrastructure, and a range of water sports including some of East Africa's best kitesurfing. The beach itself—white sand, swaying palms, warm Indian Ocean—delivers on tropical expectations.

Use Diani as a base for exploring Shimba Hills National Reserve (elephants and sable antelope) or snorkeling the marine parks of Kisite-Mpunguti.

Developed Tourism Water Sports Family Friendly 4-7 Days Ideal

Tanzania's Coastal Treasures

Zanzibar (Unguja Island)

Zanzibar needs little introduction. Stone Town's atmospheric lanes lead past spice markets, rooftop restaurants, and the waterfront where dhows have docked for centuries. Beyond the historic capital, the island offers beaches for every preference: the party scene at Nungwi, quiet seclusion at Matemwe, and the stunning sandbanks of the east coast.

Don't miss the spice farms that gave Zanzibar its name ("Spice Islands"), the endangered red colobus monkeys of Jozani Forest, and sunset dhow cruises that remain the island's most romantic experience.

UNESCO Stone Town Spice Tours Diverse Beaches 5-7 Days Ideal

Pemba Island

Zanzibar's lesser-known neighbor offers what Unguja had decades ago: empty beaches, genuine isolation, and some of the Indian Ocean's finest diving. The Pemba Channel's underwater walls and abundant pelagic life draw serious divers, while the island's agricultural interior—carpeted with clove trees—provides cultural experiences absent from more developed destinations.

Pemba is not for everyone. Infrastructure remains basic, reaching the island requires additional flights or a long ferry, and nightlife is nonexistent. For those seeking untouched Africa meeting the Indian Ocean, it's unmatched.

World-Class Diving Remote Limited Development 4-5 Days Ideal

Mafia Island

The smallest of Tanzania's three main islands, Mafia remains remarkably uncommercial. The Mafia Island Marine Park protects pristine coral gardens and seasonal whale shark gatherings (October-March). Only a handful of lodges operate, ensuring uncrowded diving and the feeling of genuine discovery.

Mafia is the choice for divers and snorkelers prioritizing marine life over beach infrastructure. Land-based activities are limited, so come prepared for a water-focused experience.

Marine Park Whale Sharks Diving Focus 3-5 Days Ideal

Safari-Beach Combinations: East Africa's coast pairs naturally with safari destinations. Common combinations include the Masai Mara or Serengeti followed by Zanzibar or the Kenyan coast, allowing you to experience both savanna and ocean in a single trip.

When to Visit

The Swahili Coast enjoys a tropical climate moderated by ocean breezes:

  • June-October: Dry season with pleasant temperatures and calm seas. Peak tourist season, especially July-August.
  • November-December: Short rains. Brief afternoon showers, lush landscapes, and fewer visitors. Good value.
  • January-February: Hot and dry. Excellent beach weather, whale shark season on Mafia.
  • March-May: Long rains. Many lodges close, particularly in Lamu. Avoid unless specifically seeking solitude.

Getting Around

Domestic flights connect major coastal destinations efficiently:

  • Nairobi to Lamu: Daily flights (2 hours)
  • Nairobi to Mombasa/Diani: Multiple daily flights (1 hour)
  • Nairobi/Arusha to Zanzibar: Daily flights (1-2 hours)
  • Zanzibar to Pemba/Mafia: Small aircraft connections

Between coastal destinations, ferries connect Lamu islands, while fast ferries link Dar es Salaam with Zanzibar (1.5 hours). Road travel between Kenya and Tanzania requires border crossings that can add significant time.

Where to Stay

Kenya Coast

  • Peponi Hotel, Lamu: Legendary Shela waterfront property. The sundeck defines Lamu style.
  • The Majlis, Lamu: Arabian nights elegance on Manda Island. Accessible only by boat.
  • Hemingways Watamu: Sophisticated beach resort with excellent diving access.
  • Alfajiri Villas, Diani: Private villa luxury with dedicated staff.

Tanzania Coast

  • Baraza Resort, Zanzibar: Opulent Swahili-Arabic design on Bwejuu Beach.
  • Matemwe Lodge, Zanzibar: Intimate chalets overlooking the reef.
  • Fundu Lagoon, Pemba: Barefoot luxury on a private peninsula.
  • Chole Mjini, Mafia: Tree-house eco-lodge with exceptional snorkeling.

Swahili Coast Essentials

  • Dress codes: Away from resort beaches, modest dress shows respect for local Muslim communities
  • Language: Swahili (Kiswahili) is the coastal lingua franca. Basic greetings are appreciated.
  • Currency: Kenyan Shillings (Kenya), Tanzanian Shillings (Tanzania), USD widely accepted at hotels
  • Health: Malaria prophylaxis recommended. Consult travel health providers before departure.
